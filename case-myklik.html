01 — Challenge

Power without
complexity

MyKlik was conceived as a tool for operations and business teams — people who know exactly what data they need to manage, but who aren't developers. The challenge was massive: how do you design a platform powerful enough to replace custom internal tools, yet simple enough that a non-technical person can use it confidently on day one?

Early prototypes leaned too heavily on developer metaphors — schemas, queries, relations — which created anxiety in user testing with non-technical participants. Meanwhile, a fully simplified version frustrated power users who needed fine-grained control.

The solution required progressive disclosure: a design that meets beginners where they are, while always offering more depth for those who want it — without one group compromising the experience of the other.

Design Principle

"The platform should feel like a spreadsheet with superpowers, not a database with a friendly face."

02 — Process

Research to
release

Discovery
User Archetypes
Mapped 3 distinct user archetypes — the Operator, the Analyst, and the Admin — each with different goals, vocabularies, and tolerance for complexity.
Benchmarks
Market Analysis
Deep analysis of Airtable, Notion, Retool, and Appsmith — identifying the design patterns that work across skill levels and those that only serve developers.
Systems
Component Library
Designed a modular component system of 40+ UI primitives — tables, forms, charts, filters — each configurable via a property panel rather than code.
Iteration
5 Testing Rounds
Ran 5 rounds of usability testing across beta users from operations, finance, and HR teams — iterating on vocabulary, onboarding, and the drag-and-drop builder.
03 — Solution

Design
principles

Principle 01
Show, don't configure
Every action has a visible, immediate result. Changes to components are reflected live on the canvas — no "preview" mode required. Confidence comes from seeing.
Principle 02
Depth on demand
Advanced options exist but are never shown by default. A beginner and an expert see the same interface — but the expert knows where to look for more control.
Principle 03
Business language
We use business terms, not technical ones. "Connect your data" instead of "define schema." "Share with team" instead of "set permissions." Language shapes confidence.
